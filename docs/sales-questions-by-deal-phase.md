# Sales Questions by Deal Phase — the evidence-based playbook

**Scope:** what questions top sellers actually ask to *build trust, discover needs, resolve
objections, and progress deals* — grounded in **hard data and peer-reviewed science**, not sales-guru
folklore. Company-agnostic; tune wording, ICP, and voice to the active profile (`PROFILE.md`,
`knowledge/voice.md`, `icp-personas.md`). Organized by deal phase so it maps onto the pipeline.

> This is a *method* doc. It cites the primary research inline and grades the strength of each source
> (see the evidence ladder below). Where popular advice contradicts the data, this doc says so.

---

## 0. The evidence ladder (read this first)

"Sales science" is a mix of genuinely rigorous work and confident opinion. This doc only uses the
first kind, and tags every claim so you know how much weight it bears:

| Grade | What it means | Sources used here |
|---|---|---|
| **[A] Peer-reviewed experiment** | Controlled studies, published, replicable | Huang et al. 2017 (*J. Personality & Social Psychology*); Brooks & John 2018 (*HBR*, summarizing behavioral-science experiments); Fiske/Cuddy warmth–competence |
| **[B] Large-n conversation analytics** | Millions of *recorded* calls, machine-analyzed. Correlational (winners *also* do X), not proof X causes winning — but samples are huge and findings replicate | Gong Labs |
| **[C] Structured field research** | Large samples, serious method, some controlled experiments; not peer-reviewed | Rackham/Huthwaite (SPIN); CEB/Gartner (Challenger); Dixon & McKenna (JOLT); RAIN Group |

**The single most important caveat:** grades B and C are largely *observational*. They tell you what
winning sellers do, not that copying it will make you win. Treat them as strong priors to test in
your own funnel, not as laws. The grade-A work is where causal claims are safest.

### What the data says that contradicts common "influencer" advice

- **"Always be closing / close hard."** In large sales, aggressive closing *reduces* success — the
  bigger the deal, the more a hard close raises buyer suspicion. [C: Rackham]
- **"Win by being the most likable relationship-builder."** In complex B2B, the *Relationship
  Builder* is the **lowest**-performing seller profile; only ~7% of star performers fit it. [C: CEB]
- **"Open-ended questions are the secret."** Rackham's data found the open-vs-closed distinction had
  **no measurable effect** on outcomes in major sales. *What* you ask (the four SPIN types) beats
  *how* you phrase it. [C: Rackham]
- **"Most deals are lost to a competitor."** They aren't — **40–60% of lost deals die in "no
  decision,"** i.e., customer indecision, not a competitive loss. [C: Dixon & McKenna]
- **"Overcome the objection fast."** Top performers do the opposite: they **pause ~5× longer** after
  an objection and talk *less*. [B: Gong]
- **"Build value first, reveal price last."** Win rates are **~10% higher when price comes up on the
  first call**, not when it's deferred. [B: Gong]

## Phase 1 — Prospecting & the opening call (goal: build trust, earn the conversation)

**What the data says.** Trust is judged *before* competence, and it is judged fast. In social
cognition, people evaluate **warmth (trustworthiness/intent) before competence (ability)**, and warmth
is the primary channel of influence — lead with it, or your competence reads as a threat [A: Fiske
Stereotype-Content Model; Cuddy, Kohut & Neffinger, *Connect, Then Lead*, HBR 2013]. The cheapest,
most reliable way to be liked in a first conversation is simply to **ask questions — especially
follow-up questions.** Across three studies of live dyadic conversations, people who asked *more
questions*, and follow-up questions in particular, were **better liked**; in a speed-dating field
study, asking more follow-up questions predicted **more second dates**. The mechanism: questions
signal *responsiveness* (listening, understanding, care) and pull *self-disclosure*, which the partner
finds enjoyable and credits to you [A: Huang, Yeomans, Brooks, Minson & Gino 2017]. And rapport still
matters commercially — RAIN Group's study of 700+ B2B purchases found the connection buyers feel
"sets the table" and is the foundation for trust — **but it is table stakes, not the differentiator**
(see Phase 4) [C: RAIN Group].

**Questions to ask** (keep the opener short; earn the right to go deeper with follow-ups):

- "What made you take the call / reply?" *(surfaces the real trigger; a follow-up hook)* [A]
- "Before I tell you why I reached out — what's top of mind for your team this quarter?" [A]
- "Can you walk me through how you handle **[relevant process]** today?" *(invites self-disclosure)* [A]
- "That's interesting — **why** is that?" / "Say more about that." *(the follow-up move that does most of the trust work)* [A: follow-up questions]
- "What would make this 20 minutes well spent for you?" *(sets a buyer-owned agenda)* [B: Gong — best openers frame value early]
- "Who else is wrestling with this on your side?" *(begins multi-threading early — see Phase 6)* [B]

**Don'ts (evidence-based):** don't open with a feature monologue, and don't over-ask — Huang et al.
caution that *too many* questions, or rude/repetitive ones, backfires. The gain is from *genuine
follow-ups*, not an interrogation.

## Phase 2 — Discovery (goal: discover needs)

**What the data says.** This is the most heavily studied phase, and the findings are unusually
consistent:

- **Ask 11–14 *targeted* questions.** Gong's analysis of **519,000** B2B call recordings found a
  clear relationship between the number of questions on a discovery call and success, peaking at
  **11–14**. Fewer than 11 = too shallow; more than 14 = diminishing returns (feels like an
  interrogation). Crucially, it's *targeted* questions — about the buyer's **business issues,
  challenges, goals, and areas of concern** — that move deals, not question volume for its own sake.
  [B: Gong]
- **Talk less.** The "golden ratio" from Gong Labs is roughly **43% talking / 57% listening** for
  top closers (their highest quota-crushers land near **46:54**); weak reps talk ~65–68%. Listening
  correlates with winning. [B: Gong]
- **Uncover need with structure, not charm.** Rackham's 12-year study of **35,000 sales calls**
  (10,000 reps, 23 countries) found the best reps ask smart, sequenced questions that lead buyers to
  *articulate their own needs* — using four question types (**S-P-I-N**). Situation and Problem
  questions open the need; **Implication** and **Need-payoff** questions (Phase 4) are what actually
  correlate with winning large deals. [C: Rackham]

**Questions to ask** — SPIN's *Situation* and *Problem* tiers do the need-discovery work here:

*Situation (context — ask few; buyers tire of these fast):* [C]
- "How is your team structured around **[function]** today?"
- "What tools/process are you using for **[job-to-be-done]** now?"
- "How long has it worked that way, and what's changed recently?"

*Problem (surface the pain — where discovery earns its keep):* [C]
- "Where does **[current approach]** break down or slow you down?"
- "How happy are you with **[metric/outcome]** as it stands?"
- "What's the hardest part of **[process]** for your team?"
- "How often does **[failure mode]** actually happen?"

*Targeted business-issue questions (the Gong "these move deals" set):* [B]
- "What are you trying to accomplish this quarter/year, and how is that measured?"
- "What's the cost — in time, money, or risk — of leaving this as-is?"
- "What's driving the urgency to look at this **now** vs. six months ago?"
- "If you solved this, what becomes possible that isn't today?"

**Technique that raises quality [A: Brooks & John 2018]:** ask **follow-up questions** and let the
buyer's *answer* choose your next question (don't run a fixed script top-to-bottom). Follow-ups
extract more, build more trust, and cost nothing.

## Phase 3 — Qualification (goal: progress the *right* deals; disqualify the rest)

**What the data says.** The expensive failure mode isn't losing to a rival — it's pouring cycles into
deals that end in **no decision (40–60% of losses)** [C: Dixon & McKenna, JOLT]. Qualification is
about detecting that early: is there a real, funded, owned decision, and are the right people in the
room? Gong's deal-level data flags **stakeholder engagement** and **defined next steps** as the
strongest health signals — single-threaded deals with vague next steps are the ones that stall [B:
Gong]. Note there's no peer-reviewed RCT behind qualification checklists (MEDDIC etc.); the *support*
for asking these questions is the JOLT/Gong evidence on where deals actually die.

**Questions to ask:**

*Decision process & authority (uncover the real path):*
- "Walk me through how a decision like this typically gets made here — who's involved, in what order?"
- "Besides you, who needs to be comfortable with this before it moves?" *(multi-threading — [B])*
- "Has your organization bought something like this before? How did that go?"
- "What could get in the way internally, even if you personally love it?"

*Metrics, economics, timing:*
- "How will you measure whether this worked, six months in?"
- "Is there budget allocated, or is this something we'd need to build a case for?"
- "What's the compelling event or deadline driving the timeline — and what happens if it slips?"

*The disqualify-honestly question [C: JOLT — surfacing indecision early]:*
- "It's completely fine if the answer is 'not now' — is this a real priority this quarter, or
  something you're exploring?"

## Phase 4 — Building the value case (goal: discover *and amplify* needs; make the cost of inaction real)

**What the data says.** This is where SPIN's core empirical finding lives. Rackham showed that in
**major** sales, the questions that separate winners are **Implication** questions (make the buyer
feel the size/cost of the problem) and **Need-payoff** questions (make the buyer articulate the value
of solving it). Implication questions work by leveraging the pain of the status quo; need-payoff
questions get the *buyer* — not you — to state the benefits, which is far more persuasive than a
seller's pitch [C: Rackham]. This is also where RAIN Group's #1 differentiator applies: **sales
winners are ~3× more likely than runners-up to bring the buyer a new idea or perspective** — teaching,
not just responding [C: RAIN Group]. It echoes CEB's *Challenger* finding: the top profile wins by
**Teach → Tailor → Take control**, reframing the buyer's thinking rather than out-charming rivals [C:
CEB/Gartner, ~6,000 reps, 90 companies; ~40% of stars are Challengers].

**Questions to ask:**

*Implication (dramatize the problem — the highest-leverage questions in the study):* [C]
- "What effect does **[problem]** have on **[downstream metric/team/customer]**?"
- "How much is that costing you — in hours, dollars, or missed revenue — over a year?"
- "If this isn't fixed before **[compelling event]**, what happens?"
- "Who else feels the pain when this goes wrong, and how does it affect *them*?"
- "Has this ever caused **[knock-on failure]**? How often?"

*Need-payoff (let the buyer sell themselves the solution):* [C]
- "If you could **[eliminate X]**, what would that be worth to you?"
- "Why is solving this important to you specifically?"
- "How would it help if you could **[capability]**?"
- "What would it mean for your team if **[outcome]** were true six months from now?"

*Teaching / reframe (bring the new idea — the RAIN/Challenger differentiator):* [C]
- "Most teams in your position assume **[common belief]** — but the data we see suggests
  **[contrarian insight]**. How does that land against your experience?"
- "Can I show you something surprising we found in accounts like yours?"

## Phase 5 — Objection handling (goal: resolve objections without pushing)

**What the data says.** Gong analyzed **67,149** sales calls at objection moments. The top finding is
counterintuitive: successful reps **pause ~5× longer immediately after** a customer objection than
weaker reps, and they **talk less** during these moments (top closers ~43% vs. average ~65%). The move
is *listen, don't leap* [B: Gong]. From negotiation science, former FBI lead negotiator Chris Voss's
**calibrated questions** ("how"/"what" questions that hand the counterpart the illusion of control and
make *them* solve the problem) are the tool of choice — they "educate your counterpart on what the
problem is rather than causing conflict by telling them" [C: Voss, *Never Split the Difference*].
Pair with **labeling** ("It sounds like the concern is…") and **mirroring** (repeat their last 1–3
words as a question) to keep them talking.

**Questions to ask** (after the pause):

*Clarify before you answer (resist the reflex to rebut):*
- "Help me understand — what specifically is giving you pause?" [B: pause + probe]
- "When you say **[their word]…?"** *(mirror — keeps them disclosing)* [C: Voss]
- "It sounds like **[fear/concern]** is the real issue here — is that right?" *(label)* [C: Voss]

*Calibrated / "make them solve it" questions:* [C: Voss]
- "How am I supposed to make this work within **[their constraint]**?"
- "What about this **isn't** working for you?"
- "What would need to be true for this to be an easy yes?"
- "How would you like to proceed from here?"

*Price/value objection (data says price early, quantify the cost of inaction):* [B: Gong / C: Rackham]
- "Compared to what — the cost of doing nothing, or another option?"
- "If price weren't the issue, is this the right solution? Let's separate those two."
- "Earlier you said **[implication cost]** — how does that weigh against the investment?"

## Phase 6 — Advancing & closing (goal: progress deals; overcome indecision)

**What the data says.** Two data points dominate this phase:

1. **Concrete next steps are decisive.** The reps who close **fastest discuss next steps 53% more**,
   and top performers pre-plan **2–3 specific, purposeful next steps before** the meeting rather than
   asking "so what should we do next?" at the end. Vague next steps = stalled deals [B: Gong].
2. **The enemy is indecision, not the competitor.** With 40–60% of losses going to "no decision," the
   winning move is **not** more pressure — it's **taking the risk off the table** for a buyer whose
   real fear is *failing*, not *missing out*. Dixon & McKenna's JOLT method (from **2.5 million**
   recorded conversations): **J**udge the indecision, **O**ffer a recommendation, **L**imit the
   exploration (don't drown them in options), **T**ake risk off the table. High performers *make a
   recommendation* instead of endlessly presenting options [C: Dixon & McKenna].

And remember Rackham: in large deals, **hard closing backfires** — earn commitment gradually [C].

**Questions / moves to use:**

*Advance with a concrete proposed next step (propose, then confirm — don't open-endedly ask):* [B]
- "Here's what I'd suggest as the next step: **[specific action, owner, date]**. Does that work, or
  is there a better path on your side?"
- "To keep this moving, I'd want to get **[stakeholder]** in the room by **[date]** — can we set that
  now?"

*Overcome indecision (JOLT — de-risk, don't push):* [C]
- "Based on everything you've told me, here's exactly what I'd do in your shoes: **[clear
  recommendation]**." *(Offer a recommendation — [C])*
- "What's the one thing that, if it went wrong, would make this a mistake for you?" *(names the fear
  of failure directly — the core JOLT lever)*
- "What would have to be true for you to feel confident deciding by **[date]**?"
- "Would a **[pilot / opt-out clause / phased rollout / success guarantee]** make this feel safer?"
  *(Take risk off the table — [C])*

*Calibrated close (hand them control) [C: Voss]:*
- "How should we move forward from here?"
- "What's the best way to get this across the line on your side?"

## Quick reference — one line per phase

| Phase | Goal | The evidence-backed move | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening | Build trust | Lead with warmth; ask **follow-up questions** (↑ liking) | A |
| 2. Discovery | Discover needs | **11–14 targeted** questions; talk ~43%, listen ~57% | B |
| 3. Qualify | Progress right deals | Map decision process + **multi-thread**; disqualify honestly | B/C |
| 4. Value case | Amplify need | **Implication + Need-payoff** questions; bring a new idea (3× winner trait) | C |
| 5. Objections | Resolve objections | **Pause 5× longer**, talk less; **calibrated "how/what"** questions | B/C |
| 6. Advance/close | Progress the deal | **Concrete next steps** (+53%); **de-risk indecision**, don't hard-close | B/C |

### How to read this file critically

Grades **B** and **C** are the backbone of "sales science," but almost all of it is **observational and
often vendor-produced** — it shows correlation (winners *also* ask more implication questions), not
proof that the tactic *causes* the win. The grade-**A** psychology (question-asking → liking; warmth →
influence) is where causal claims are safest, but it's about *conversations in general*, not B2B deals
specifically. The honest synthesis: these are **strong, converging priors** — the closest thing the
field has to hard data — that you should **A/B test in your own funnel** rather than treat as
guarantees. That skeptical posture is exactly what the sources themselves support.
